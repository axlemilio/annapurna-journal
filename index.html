Children of the Korn in Annapurna

A Family Journey through Nepal

Arrival in Kathmandu

Stepping off the plane in Kathmandu, I was hit by a wall of sensation: incense and diesel fumes, prayer flags and tuk‑tuks swirling in the same narrow streets. Our journey from Manila through Kuala Lumpur had deposited us in this chaotic, ancient capital at the start of the dry season. Why did we come? Because after years of pushing through start‑up grind, I longed to stand beneath mountains older than memory. The guidebooks told me to expect cacophony; what I hadn’t expected was how alive it would make me feel. That first evening we climbed to a rooftop café as the sun sank behind the valley, the sky turning amber over Durbar Square and the Monkey Temple. Sipping sweet milk tea, I felt my mind slow to the pace of the prayer wheels spinning below.

To Pokhara & the Eco Village

After two days exploring Patan’s courtyards and the temples of Swayambhunath, we boarded a tiny plane to Pokhara, the gateway to Annapurna. We had written to several guides about treks to Mardi Himal or Poon Hill. But by the time we reached Pokhara, our children were fighting fevers. The idea of dragging them up three‑thousand‑metre passes suddenly felt cruel. Over breakfast by Phewa Lake we decided to change course. An old friend told us of an eco‑village a few hours’ drive from town where life moved to the rhythm of farmers and birdsong. A jeep bumped us up a dirt road to a cluster of stone cottages strung with prayer flags. Under the snow‑peaked gaze of Machapuchare we found a home we didn’t know we were seeking.

Life at the Eco Village

Days at the eco‑village settled into a gentle rhythm. We woke to the calls of roosters and the laughter of children doing yoga on the grassy terrace. After a breakfast of homemade cornflakes and chapati we would wander down to the school. The local girls taught my daughter how to spin and dance; they giggled at my attempts to imitate their steps and insisted we keep dancing until we were all breathless. One afternoon the owner led us into a field where chamomile bloomed in yellow patches. We spent an hour picking flowers and listening to her stories about the village’s beginnings. Later we learned to churn butter in the farmhouse kitchen while her mother smiled with the patient pride of someone who has repeated the same motion ten thousand times. Evenings were spent around a crackling fireplace, trading tales with travellers and sipping tea infused with mountain herbs. It was here that my son proclaimed he would open a tea shop when he grew up.

Mountain Views & Hikes

As our children recovered, we ventured further afield. Short hikes led us through terraced fields and rhododendron forests, up to clearings where prayer flags fluttered against impossibly blue skies. I had dreamed of standing face‑to‑face with the Himalaya, and there they were: Machapuchare’s fishtail peak piercing the sky, the Annapurnas stretching in jagged procession. Sunrise painted the snow pink; sunset drenched the hills in gold. One afternoon an old man—he insisted we call him Baba—took us along a trail to a ridgetop. He walked with the agility of someone half his age, laughing as he pointed out medicinal plants. At the summit he sat, silent, gazing at the mountains. ‘They change you,’ he said simply. Looking out at that vastness, I felt a quiet dissolving of all the petty worries I had carried up there.

Beyond the Village

The week slipped by. On the way back to Kathmandu we stopped at a roadside restaurant overlooking a river and ate steaming momos while hawks circled overhead. Back in the city we surrendered once more to the thrum of Thamel. We shopped for beads in a tiny alleyway, haggled for singing bowls, and feasted on dal bhat and millet pancakes. A menu from a layover in Kuala Lumpur now seems almost absurd—a reminder of how far we had come in such a short time. In the quiet of our hotel room I thought about the trek we didn’t take. I expected disappointment but felt none. The mountains had given us what we needed, even if we never set foot on their slopes.

Reflections & Farewell

On our last night at the eco‑village, the locals invited us to a dance party. We twirled under stars we’d never seen before, our movements echoing those of the first evening’s rooftop dance but richer now, layered with shared stories. In the morning we said our goodbyes over tea and pancakes. I left with a respect for the people who call Annapurna home—the mothers who smile as they churn butter, the grandfathers who roam mountains like goats, the children whose laughter is a melody. I also left with a quieter heart. The Himalayas didn’t just dwarf my problems; they taught me that sometimes the most meaningful journeys are the ones that lead you inward. We came seeking peaks but found connection. As the plane lifted off from Kathmandu, prayer flags flapping below, I closed my eyes and felt the mountains within me.

If You Go

Essential gear for Annapurna adventures includes a good raincoat, a warm jacket, gloves, a hat, sunglasses, sun cream and a refillable water bottle. We would also recommend slow trekking with an experienced guide — Ram of Essence Tours helped us plan our itinerary and offered invaluable advice, while Dambar K.C. of Sisne Rover provided a family‑friendly Poon Hill itinerary. Annapurna Eco‑Village can arrange transport from Pokhara and welcomes guests of all ages.
